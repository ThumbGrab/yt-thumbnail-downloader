How Much Does YouTube Pay Per 1,000 Views in 2026?

A clear, honest breakdown of what YouTube actually pays — and the factors that make your earnings higher or lower.

📅 Updated January 2026 · ⏱️ 7 min read · By the ThumbnailGrab Team

It's one of the most-asked questions for anyone starting on YouTube: how much money do you actually make per 1,000 views? The honest answer is that it varies a lot — but there are clear ranges and rules that explain why. This guide breaks down the real numbers, the terms you need to know, and what makes some channels earn far more than others for the same number of views.

The Short Answer

For most channels, YouTube ad revenue works out to roughly $1 to $5 per 1,000 views after YouTube takes its share. Some niches earn well below that, and a few high-value niches earn much more. So a video with 100,000 views might earn anywhere from around $100 to $500 — or occasionally more or less, depending on the factors below.

That figure is what's called your RPM, and understanding it is the key to everything else.

RPM vs CPM: What's the Difference?

These two terms get confused constantly, so let's make them simple:

  • CPM (Cost Per Mille) is what advertisers pay per 1,000 ad impressions. This is the "gross" number before YouTube's cut.
  • RPM (Revenue Per Mille) is what actually lands in your pocket per 1,000 video views, after YouTube keeps its 45% share and after accounting for views that didn't show ads.

RPM is the number that matters to you as a creator, because it reflects your real take-home earnings. CPM is interesting, but RPM is your reality.

What Affects How Much You Earn

Two channels with the exact same view count can earn wildly different amounts. Here's why:

1. Your Niche

This is the single biggest factor. Advertisers pay more to reach audiences likely to buy expensive things. Finance, business, tech, and software channels often see high RPMs, while entertainment, gaming, and kids' content typically see lower ones — simply because of what advertisers will pay for those viewers.

2. Your Audience Location

Views from countries with higher advertising markets (like the US, UK, Canada, and Australia) generally earn more than views from regions where ad rates are lower. A channel with mostly US viewers can earn several times more per view than one with the same views from low-CPM regions.

3. Video Length and Ad Placement

Videos over 8 minutes can carry multiple ads, which can increase earnings per view. Where and how ads appear also matters, though it's wise not to overload viewers with ads at the expense of watch time.

4. Time of Year

Advertising budgets swing through the year. Rates often peak in Q4 (October–December) around the holiday shopping season and dip in January when budgets reset. The same video can earn noticeably different amounts depending on when those views happen.

Realistic Earnings Examples

Here's roughly what different view counts might earn at a few sample RPM levels. Treat these as ballpark ranges, not promises — your real numbers depend on everything above.

1,000$1$3$5

As you can see, the same million views could mean $1,000 or $5,000 depending mostly on niche and audience. This is why two creators with identical view counts can have very different incomes.

💡 Tip: Ad revenue is only one income stream. Many established creators earn more from sponsorships, affiliate links, memberships, and their own products than from YouTube ads alone.

How to Estimate Your Own Earnings

A simple formula gets you a rough estimate:

Estimated earnings = (Views ÷ 1,000) × your RPM
Example: 50,000 views × $2 RPM ÷ 1,000 = about $100

You won't know your real RPM until you're in the YouTube Partner Program and can see it in YouTube Studio. Until then, $1–$5 is a reasonable planning range for most niches.

The Bottom Line

YouTube pays most creators somewhere between $1 and $5 per 1,000 views, with niche and audience location driving most of the difference. Don't fixate on the per-view number — focus on making videos people genuinely want to watch, growing your views, and adding other income streams over time. The earnings follow the audience.
